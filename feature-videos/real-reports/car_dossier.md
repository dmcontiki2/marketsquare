**On Recalls:**

The current active Toyota SA Takata airbag recall affects the Hilux **2004–2015** range only — the 2019 model is **not** affected by this recall. All brands with active campaigns have dedicated website sections where owners can supply their VINs to check if they are affected. **Action required:** Supply the VIN to Toyota SA (toyota.co.za/owners/toyota-recall) or call a dealer to confirm no outstanding campaigns apply to this specific chassis. The NRCS Recall Hotline is 080 111 999 (toll-free).

**Key forum-corroborated risk at this mileage:** At least five documented cases of exhaust manifold gasket failure on the 2.8, with one resulting in head replacement — heat from the exhaust damages the head if the blown gasket is ignored. Toyota SA released a technical service bulletin (not a full recall) for 2.8 GD-6 vehicles presenting with blown gaskets, instructing dealers to replace with an improved manifold and gasket from the newer 150 kW spec. **Check whether this TSB work has been done.**

## 4. Cost of Ownership

### Service Schedule
The Hilux 2.8 GD-6 requires nine services across 90,000 km at 10,000 km intervals; these services can be spread over up to nine years provided the vehicle is serviced at least once every 12 months. At 120,000 km, this vehicle is well beyond the standard factory service plan window (which covered 9 services/90,000 km). All future servicing is at the owner's cost.

Toyota recommends servicing the Hilux every 15,000 km or 12 months, whichever comes first; in harsh South African conditions (dusty roads, stop-start traffic, extreme heat), shorter intervals of around 10,000 km are beneficial.

### Insurance

The Hilux is considered a high risk for theft by car insurance companies — Toyotas are the second-most stolen car brand in South Africa according to the Ctrack Vehicle Theft Report. Toyota Hilux models are the best-selling vehicle in South Africa, and Toyota has made support and service a priority, which may positively affect the cost of comprehensive cover. Expect comprehensive insurance premiums in the **R1,800–R3,500/month** range (ESTIMATE) depending on area, driver profile, and tracker fitment — this is not verified by a live insurer quote. Obtain at least three quotes via Hippo.co.za or King Price before committing.

### Parts Availability

There are close to 200 Toyota dealerships in South Africa offering the full Hilux range. OEM parts are widely stocked. Aftermarket parts are available from suppliers such as Gemini Parts (Johannesburg) and Caryota Spare (Cape Town). The 2.8 GD-6 is South Africa's most popular bakkie engine — parts availability is **excellent** at all price points from OEM to stripping yard.

### Fuel Reality

Fuel consumption is around 7.2–7.6 L/100 km combined in tested variants (2025/26 tune). The 2019 130 kW tune should achieve 7.5–9.0 L/100 km depending on load, terrain and driving style — rural highway use will be more economical; heavy towing or off-road use will push toward 10–12 L/100 km. Use quality diesel (avoid suspect informal filling stations) — fuel quality in some SA regions can accelerate injector wear.

## 5. Test-Drive Checklist

**Before you start the engine:**
1. Inspect the exhaust manifold area for carbon soot streaking — a known GD-6 fault; press your finger along the manifold joint seam and check for black carbon deposits.
2. Check the airbox/intake for signs of dust on the clean (post-filter) side — dust ingestion through the filter element is a documented issue; check the MAF sensor for contamination.
3. Inspect underneath for oil leaks at the gearbox/engine junction — an oil leak at the gearbox/engine interface was a documented fault on GD-6 builds.
4. Check chassis rails, load bay floor, and diff housings for off-road damage, welding or bending.
5. **Demand the full stamped service book** — check every 10,000 km service stamp is present. For a 2019 model at 120,000 km, you want 12+ stamps. Any gap >15,000 km without explanation is a red flag.

**Cold-start check:**
6. Start the engine cold — listen for excessive smoke (white smoke = possible coolant ingestion; black = rich running/DPF; blue = oil burning).
7. Let it idle: listen for the ticking of a blown exhaust manifold gasket — especially under light throttle blip.

**On the move:**
8. Drive at highway speed (80–100 km/h) for at least 20 minutes — this is when the DPF should trigger passive regeneration; watch for limp mode or power drop-off. Regular highway driving at 80+ km/h for 20–30 minutes is essential for DPF health.
9. Accelerate hard from low revs in 3rd gear — the VGT turbo should spool cleanly with no hesitation; both generations use a variable-geometry turbo controlled by an electronic stepper-motor actuator — symptoms of failure are sudden power loss and the engine refusing to rev past ~1,500 rpm.
10. Test 4WD engagement (if 4x4 variant): engage 4H and 4L at crawling speed on a safe surface — the transfer should engage without flashing lights or grinding. A corroded earth point above the gearbox can cause the 4WD light to flash but the system not to engage.
11. Manual only: test each gear including reverse for crunch; check clutch biting point is mid-pedal, not at the top (sign of wear).
12. Auto only: check for delayed or harsh shifts at 40–80 km/h under moderate acceleration; confirm gearbox fluid is red/pink (not brown/burnt smelling).

**After the drive:**
13. Let the engine idle to operating temp — any coolant smell or steam from the cooling system warrants an immediate compression/leak-down test.
14. Plug in an OBD-II scanner (ask for one or bring your own) — read live data and scan for stored codes, particularly P2002 (DPF efficiency below threshold), P0400-range (EGR), P0299 (turbo underboost), and P0202/P0204-range (injector faults).

**Paperwork to demand:**
- Full, original stamped service book (every 10,000 km stamp)
- Full service history invoices — confirm DPF inspection, fuel filter changes
- NATIS (registration certificate) and current roadworthy (COF/RWC)
- Written confirmation of any TSB/recall work done (specifically the manifold TSB)
- Accident/damage history: obtain a TransUnion/VehicleCheck/AutoTrader History report on the VIN
- Finance clearance certificate if seller is a private individual with outstanding finance

## 6. Negotiation Lens

**Lever 1 — Imminent DPF/EGR service risk (strongest lever)**
DPF blockage from town-only driving never lets the exhaust get hot enough to regenerate the filter; a blocked DPF costs R2,000 to clean up to R40,000 to replace. Unless the seller can produce documentary evidence of recent DPF health checks and a predominantly highway-use profile, table this as a R5,000–R15,000 contingency and request it be deducted from the asking price.

**Lever 2 — Exhaust manifold TSB (unverified until you check)**
The exhaust manifold gasket failure is a documented multi-vehicle fault on the 2.8; it has led to head replacements when ignored. If the improved manifold and gasket TSB work cannot be confirmed in the paperwork, quote the dealer/seller a repair estimate of R5,000–R60,000 depending on head damage, and ask for a price concession or written warranty that the work has been done.

**Lever 3 — New-generation Hilux market pressure**
The ninth-generation 2026 Hilux has been fully revealed, with the locally produced version from Toyota's Prospecton plant scheduled to arrive in South Africa during 2026. New stock of fresh-generation Hiluxes is entering the market now, which will suppress resale values of 2019 examples over the next 12–18 months. Use this as a time-pressure counter-argument if the seller is inflexible — the longer they wait, the more the market moves against them.

**Target negotiated price: R385,000–R405,000** for a clean, fully stamped double cab 4x4 Legend/Raider. Walk away if service history is incomplete, DPF history is unknown, and the seller won't move below R410,000.
